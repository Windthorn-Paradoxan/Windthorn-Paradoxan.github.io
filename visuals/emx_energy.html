Zero basin, polarity flows, and loops that sustain themselves without breaking conservation.

1. Energy Reservoir & Polarity Flow

Zero Basin with ± Flow

In EMx terms, any system that “lives” over time has a reservoir tied to a signed-zero basin: a central state that represents balanced potential. From there, energy is pushed into positive modes (activity, expression, work) and pulled back into negative modes (cooling, rest, dissipation).

Every action is a polarity move: +0 is structure-building, −0 is structure-relaxing, 0 is the still point that keeps the book balanced. The system survives by never letting its reservoir fall below the minimum needed to recover equilibrium.

This is energy management: how far you can push away from 0 before you must either renew, collapse, or reconfigure.

No magic energy creation — just disciplined shuttling between +0, 0, and −0.

2. Renewal: Spend → Decay → Refill → Recenter

Four-Phase Renewal Loop

EMx likes four-fold cycles: spend, decay, refill, recenter. Any living system or engine that runs for a long time must have all four.

  • Spend (+0) – energy is pushed outward into structure, motion, cognition, or work.
  • Decay (−0) – structures loosen, entropy increases, waste builds up, tension bleeds away.
  • Refill (input) – external sources (food, light, information, etc.) top up the basin to workable levels.
  • Recenter (0) – the system re-balances; thresholds and gains are adjusted before the next cycle.

If any phase is missing, the loop breaks: pure spending burns out, pure decay collapses, pure refill bloats, and pure stillness stagnates.

EMx-style: loop = [+0 spend] → [−0 decay] → [input] → [0 recenter] → repeat
spend decay refill recenter

3. Self-Propagation: Loops That Keep Going

When a System Can Propagate Itself

Self-propagation in EMx is just a loop whose net gain per cycle stays above the minimum: enough energy to copy structure, repair damage, and re-center for the next round.

Instead of “infinite growth”, EMx cares about stable replication loops: patterns that can recur without violating conservation or no-clone constraints. Biology is the obvious example: cells spend energy, decay, import new energy, then recenter and divide.

You can model this as a loop over states, with an energy budget attached:

self_propagate ⇔ every cycle: E_next = E_now + input − (work + loss) ≥ E_min

As long as the loop respects polarity (no free creation), no-clone (no perfect copies without cost), and basin limits (don’t drain the reservoir), the pattern can keep re-appearing — locally “immortal” even if individual pieces wear out.

4. EMx Summary of Energy & Self-Propagation

Put together, EMx describes energy management as a dance between a central 0-reservoir, excursions into +0 (work, structure, expression), balancing moves into −0 (relaxation, decay, clearance), and continuous renewal from outside.

Self-propagation isn’t a cheat code; it’s a loop whose bookkeeping actually closes: every cycle returns to a viable basin with enough reserve to rebuild, repair, and fork off a new copy of the pattern.

Whenever a system manages that — from a single cell to a whole civilization — EMx sees the same underlying structure: a lattice of states, polarity-respecting flows, and recurring loops that keep threading through the same region of state-space without running the reservoir dry.
